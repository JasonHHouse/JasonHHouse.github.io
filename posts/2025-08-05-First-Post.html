By Jason House | August 5, 2025

Giving Difficult Feedback

Situation

While at Indeed, I was coaching two engineering managers toward promotion to senior manager. Both were performing at a high level and demonstrating the ownership and leadership behaviors we needed. Unfortunately, due to budget constraints, I could only move forward with one promotion. This meant I had to give difficult feedback to a strong performer who had done everything right, but wouldn't be promoted, through no fault of their own.

Task

My goal was to deliver this feedback in a way that preserved trust, motivation, and engagement. I needed to be honest and empathetic, while also keeping this manager aligned with long-term growth and making sure he felt recognized and supported.

Action

Before the promotion cycle, I had already flagged the budget risks with both managers to manage expectations. I also helped them shape strong business cases, spending time coaching on how to frame their impact and growth. When the final decision came back, only one case was approved, with the other being rejected due to being “not business critical.” I scheduled a 1:1 with the impacted manager and delivered the news with empathy and clarity. I made it clear the decision was budgetary, not performance-based, and reaffirmed his value to the team. I shared a concrete plan to revisit the case in six months and highlighted how we could strengthen it with additional business impact. To acknowledge his contributions, I also secured additional compensation for him, which helped soften the outcome. I offered him time to process, including taking the rest of the day off if needed.

Result

He responded with professionalism and genuine support for his colleague who was promoted. He appreciated my transparency and the fact that I had gone the extra mile to advocate for him. His motivation remained high, and he continued to take on broader responsibilities.

Learning

This experience reinforced how important it is to lead with transparency, empathy, and foresight. I learned that setting expectations early, delivering difficult feedback with care, and recognizing people in multiple ways, whether through compensation or future opportunities, can help maintain morale and trust. It also reminded me that difficult conversations, when done well, can actually strengthen a team and individual commitment.
